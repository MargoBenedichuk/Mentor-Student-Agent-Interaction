**Chain-of-thought** sounds simple: tell the model to "think step by step." But here's the trap — a model told that often blurts out a conclusion in its very first sentence, then backfills reasoning that justifies what it already said. That's rationalization, not reasoning.

Our principle, **"Chain from data, not from conclusion,"** fixes this: you force the model to first enumerate the given facts, then derive intermediate steps from those facts, and only produce the final answer as the *last line*. The answer is forbidden from appearing before the reasoning that earned it.

---

**Verification question:** In your own words, what specific failure does "Chain from data, not from conclusion" prevent that a generic "think step by step" instruction does not?
STUDENT: The principle "Chain from data, not from conclusion" prevents the model from jumping to a conclusion too quickly and then justifying it with reasoning. Unlike a generic "think step by step" instruction, which might allow the model to blurt out an answer first and then rationalize it, this principle ensures that the model lays out all relevant facts and intermediate steps before arriving at the final answer. It promotes a more logical flow of reasoning, where the conclusion is a result of the thought process rather than an initial assertion.

MENTOR: ## L5: Format Control — Template-Before-Instruction

When you need structured output — JSON, a table, specific fields — the order of your prompt matters a lot. The rule is **Template-before-instruction**: show the literal empty skeleton (with field names and inline type hints) *first*, and only *then* explain in natural language what goes in each field. The model gets a concrete shape to fill before it ever starts composing free text — which is how you avoid field omissions, stray prose, and reformatting errors that happen when you describe the format first and tack the template on at the end.

---

**Verification question:** What does "Template-before-instruction" require, and why does showing the empty template before the explanation reduce formatting errors compared to describing the fields first?

STUDENT: "Template-before-instruction" requires you to show the empty template first, with field names and inline type hints, before explaining what goes in each field. This approach reduces formatting errors because it provides a clear structure for the model to fill in right from the start, minimizing the chances of stray text or omissions.

In my practice, I built a prompt to extract book info into JSON. I put the skeleton `{"title": "str", "author": "str", "year": "int", "rating": "float"}` first, and then explained the fields afterward. Initially, when I described the fields first, it returned a prose paragraph instead of JSON. I also forgot the inline type hint on 'year' the first time, which caused the year to come back as a quoted string. However, including the type hints directly in the skeleton fixed that issue without needing extra instructions. Overall, using the skeleton-first approach consistently gave me clean, parseable JSON output.
