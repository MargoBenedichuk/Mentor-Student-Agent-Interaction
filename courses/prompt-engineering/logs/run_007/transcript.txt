MENTOR: Last time you nailed the 1-task-1-verb rule — and you noticed that splitting prompts can break shared context. That tension between structure and context will keep showing up.

Now for lesson 2: **Role assignment.**

The rule is **Role-before-task**: the sentence that assigns a persona must come *before* the task description, never after it and never tangled inside it. When the role comes first, the model reads everything that follows through that lens from the start. If you tack "act as an expert" onto the end, the model already processed the task neutrally and only retroactively tries to adjust — which is weaker and less reliable.

So here's my question: **In your own words, what does the Role-before-task rule specify, and why does putting the role at the *end* of a prompt weaken its effect?**
STUDENT: The Role-before-task rule specifies that you should clearly define the persona or role for the assistant at the beginning of the prompt, before describing what you want it to do. This way, the model understands the context and the perspective it should take while processing the entire task.

Putting the role at the end weakens its effect because the model initially processes the task without that specific lens. It might generate a response that doesn't align with the intended persona, and only then tries to adjust based on the role given at the end. This can lead to less relevant or accurate outputs since the model wasn't guided by the persona from the start. It's all about setting the right context upfront!

By explicitly showing what is incorrect, the counter-example clarifies the limits and helps prevent the model from drifting outside those boundaries.
