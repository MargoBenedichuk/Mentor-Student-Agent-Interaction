**L6: Constraints**

Constraints — "do not" statements — are how you close off known failure modes. But they have a nasty property: the more you stack, the harder they are for a model to track simultaneously, and over-mentioning a forbidden behavior can actually *prime* the model to do it. So our rule is **3-negatives-max**: any single prompt gets at most three explicit prohibition constraints. If you need to close more than three failure modes, the 4th and beyond must be reframed as a *positive* instruction — tell the model what to do instead. And the three you keep should be the top-3 most likely or most costly failures for *that specific prompt*, not just the first three that come to mind.

STUDENT: The 3-negatives-max rule says you cap explicit "do not" constraints at three per prompt. The reason more than that backfires is that negative constraints keep the forbidden behavior in the model's attention — you're basically repeating the thing you don't want it to do, and that can prime it to actually do that thing. It's like saying "don't think of a pink elephant" — now that's exactly what's in your head.

Past three, you reframe the rest as positive instructions: tell the model what to do instead of what not to do.

**Iterative refinement** isn't about randomly rewriting a prompt until something sticks. STUDENT: The two steps are:

1. **Diagnosis** — you name exactly what in the *prompt* caused the bad output. Not "the answer was wrong," but something like "the instruction didn't specify a format, so the model defaulted to a paragraph instead of a list."

2. **Version** — you produce a v2 that changes *only* what the diagnosis pointed to, and you keep v1 visible alongside it.

Showing v1 next to v2 is part of the requirement because the delta is where the learning happens. If you only show the fixed version, you can't tell what actually changed — and more importantly, *you* can't tell whether your diagnosis was right.
