**The concept.** Most bad prompts fail for the same reason: they cram multiple actions into one sentence — "do X and also Y and make sure Z." The model latches onto the first clear verb and the rest becomes noise. The fix is the **"1-task-1-verb" rule**: pick exactly one imperative verb, make it the backbone of the prompt, and move every constraint or formatting note *after* it, never tangled inside. If you catch yourself writing "and" between two verbs at the top level, split into two prompts.

**My question to you:** In your own words — what does the 1-task-1-verb rule say, and why does mixing two verbs in one instruction usually go wrong?
STUDENT: The 1-task-1-verb rule emphasizes that you should focus on giving one clear instruction at a time. When you mix two verbs in a single prompt, it confuses the model because it tends to latch onto the first verb and ignore the rest. This leads to incomplete or unexpected outputs since the model might not understand which action to prioritize. Keeping it simple ensures clarity and better results!

Now, on to **Lesson 2: Role assignment.** Here's the idea:

A prompt that starts with a role — "You are a technical editor" — does more than set tone. It narrows the model's attention to a specific set of conventions, vocabulary, and priorities *before* you ever state the task. The rule here is that the role should come first in the prompt, before the instruction, and it should be a single, concrete persona — not a vague mashup like "You are an expert copywriter and also a data analyst." That's just the 1-task-1-verb problem wearing a costume.

So: **In your own words — why does putting a concrete role at the *start* of a prompt change the model's output in a way that a vague or mid-prompt role doesn't?**
STUDENT: Starting with a concrete role sets a clear context for the model right from the beginning. It helps the model understand the specific perspective, priorities, and vocabulary it should adopt for the task. When the role is vague or introduced later in the prompt, it can lead to confusion about how to approach the task, resulting in less relevant or focused outputs. A well-defined role at the start directs the model's attention and aligns its responses with expectations, improving the overall quality of the output. 

Does that align with what you've seen?
