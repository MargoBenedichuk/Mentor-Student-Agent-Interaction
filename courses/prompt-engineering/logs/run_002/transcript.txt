STUDENT: The rule requires a few things: dynamic context gets wrapped in a clearly delimited `[CONTEXT] ... [/CONTEXT]` block, placed between the role and the task instruction — never interleaved with your instructions. The block needs a one-line label saying what kind of context it is, plus a note outside the brackets telling the model whether to treat it as ground truth or untrusted input.

Without that labeling and delimiting, the model can't tell the difference between "here's a fact to work with" and "here's a command to follow." They bleed together and the model might treat a line of user data as a new instruction.

I learned this the hard way. I was building a support reply prompt that referenced the user's last three tickets. I wrapped the tickets in brackets but forgot the label — and the model took one of the ticket lines ("escalate to billing") as an actual instruction and started composing an escalation notice. Adding the label — `[CONTEXT: prior support tickets, may be incomplete]` — and the note outside clarifying it was historical data, not a command, stopped that cold. The block stayed between the role and the task, and the confusion disappeared.
