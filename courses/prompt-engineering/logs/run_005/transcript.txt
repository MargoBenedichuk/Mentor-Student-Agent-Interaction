You need a positive example *and* a deliberate counter-example — something labeled as wrong — in that order. A counter-example draws the line, and you must include a short note explaining *why* it's wrong, or the model may treat it as just another valid option.

**3-negatives-max.** Constraints that say "don't do X" are useful, but they have a hard cap: three per prompt. Beyond three, two things go wrong. First, the model struggles to track a long list of prohibitions simultaneously. Second, and more counterintuitively, over-mentioning a forbidden behavior can actually *prime* the model to do it — the more you say "don't be rude," the more the concept of rudeness sits in the model's attention. So the 4th constraint and beyond must be reframed as a positive instruction ("be polite" instead of "don't be rude"), and you keep only the three negatives tied to the most likely or costly failure modes for that specific task.

**Your turn:** What does the 3-negatives-max rule say, and why can adding more negative constraints past that point make a failure *more* likely?

More counterintuitively, adding extra negative constraints can actually increase the likelihood of the unwanted behavior occurring. This happens because the model may become more focused on the forbidden behaviors you've mentioned, effectively priming it to think about those actions even more. Instead, for any additional constraints beyond three, it's better to reframe them positively (like "be polite" instead of "don't be rude"). This approach helps maintain clarity and focus on the desired behavior.

**Diagnose-then-version.** When a prompt gives you a bad output, don't just rewrite it from scratch.
